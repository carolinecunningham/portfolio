Subtheme: Speculation

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. The MAPC report “Homes for Profit: Speculation and Investment in Greater Boston” mentions that institutional investors are often preferred given that “Cash offers are more appealing to sellers than traditional mortgages”. To understand the potential appeal of these mortgages, I want to uncover to what extent are fully-cash payments made in the Boston market. Specifically, has this trend changed over time (especially since the 2008 recession)?
  2. When on a NYC tour, I remember the guide pointing out that the highly-desirable Park Avenue apartments are often owned by foreign wealth using the apartment as an investment rather than a home. From this experience, I became curious about what regions in Boston are targeted for speculation and, specifically, are high-wealth areas preferred?
  3. The MAPC report finds “flipping to be most common in Greater Boston’s highest value submarket,” which refers to a suburban area outside Boston. Within Boston, I am curious to uncover where flips are most profitable and which communities are most impacted by this trend accordingly. I assume that based on the MAPC finding flips may be most likely in wealthier neighborhoods but this trend may not generalize.
  4. From living in my hometown, I have observed over the years older apartment buildings being fixed up and resold as luxury living apartments. From what I have learned in class, I imagine these properties were flipped and re-sold at higher prices. As such, I am curious to understand what building features (such as when a property was built) affected subsequent price increases?

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

In this speculation investigation, I examined market conditions, spatial patterns, flipping profit opportunities, and property features that may lead to greater flipping price mark-ups. I found that in the years after 2008, payments in all-cash became more common by both count and volume of money, suggesting that the financial crisis may have increased the value of a financially-secure institutional investor. Corroborating the MAPC report, I also found that wealthy areas in Boston were most targeted for flipping as well as led to the most profits, demonstrating that the trend of investing in wealthy areas holds true for Boston proper. In my analysis of what types of buildings led to the most profit as well as the greatest differences in price, the most significant conclusions were that the size of the investor seems to correspond to profit opportunities, underscoring that buyers with large financial backings can dominate the market.

In this investigation, I was reminded of the learning from class to pay attention to what data is not being collected, not just what fields are shown. While I was able to access data on buyers and sellers, what was not shown were the residents involved and larger community implications. Given the importance of neighborhoods in forming communities, better understanding the effects of transactions on the people involved is critical for any housing policy discussion.
